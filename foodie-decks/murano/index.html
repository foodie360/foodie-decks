What we saw on Instagram

A perfect feed that isn't selling

The photography is excellent
Every frame is well-lit, well-composed, and looks like fine dining. It tells people the food is serious. That's real value for someone who already found the profile.
But it doesn't humanize the place
No chef. No team. No moment. No one behind the food. Chef Ferraro is the story here, and he's invisible on the feed. Fine dining sells on trust, and trust comes from people, not plates.
Organic reach: 2 to 4% of followers
11K followers sounds strong. In practice, a post reaches 220 to 440 people, most of whom already know Murano exists. Instagram at this stage keeps you present for people already considering you. It doesn't bring in new ones.
Content without paid media stays in a closed loop
The missing piece isn't better content. It's connecting that content to a paid media system that takes it to people who don't follow the account yet, in the right radius, at the right moment.

What we see

Three things worth talking about

01
Google presence doesn't match the quality
The restaurant has earned real press. Global Miami Magazine, Sun-Sentinel. The physical experience is clearly there. But on Google, the review volume is thin for a six-month-old restaurant at this level. That's the first thing a new customer checks before making a reservation, and it's a gap that's easy to close with the right system.
02
Visibility is a gap, not a quality problem
When someone searches "Italian restaurant Hallandale Beach" or "special occasion dinner near me," the results are dominated by restaurants with years of reviews behind them. That's not a reflection of food quality. It's a reflection of time in market and paid presence. Both are closable.
03
Every empty table has a fixed cost running behind it
Rent, kitchen brigade, front of house: those costs run the same whether the room is full or half-full. At a $75+ average check, one additional table filled on a Tuesday or Wednesday isn't just revenue. It's near-pure margin. The cost of not being found isn't the marketing budget. It's the covers that didn't happen.
